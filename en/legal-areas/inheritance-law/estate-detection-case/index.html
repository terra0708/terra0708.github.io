What Is an Estate Determination Case and How Is It Opened?

Home Legal Areas Inheritance Law Estate Determination Case

This article summarizes basic information on the tereke tespiti (estate determination) proceeding under Turkish law, which arises when the full extent of the deceased’s assets is unknown, when heirs disagree on what forms part of the estate, or when the scope of the estate must be clarified before partition. This action is one of the legal tools for protecting the estate under Turkish Civil Code Article 589. Below we address what the case is, when it may be filed, who may file it, which court has jurisdiction, and how the process typically unfolds.

What is an estate determination case?

An estate determination case is a request to identify and record the deceased’s assets and liabilities after death. Through this process, immovables, bank accounts, vehicle records, receivables, payables and other components may be investigated and registered. It rests on the rules allowing the peace judge to take protective measures for the estate under Turkish Civil Code Art. 589.

When is an estate determination case filed?

It is typically filed when, among others:

  • the full extent of the deceased’s property is unknown;
  • heirs disagree;
  • there is suspicion of concealment of assets;
  • protection of estate assets is needed; or
  • the estate must be clarified before partition.

The proceeding has a protective and declaratory character.

Who may file an estate determination case?

It may generally be filed by:

  • statutory heirs;
  • appointed heirs;
  • legatees; and
  • estate creditors.

Where necessary, the peace judge may also act ex officio.

Against whom is an estate determination case “filed”?

This is not a typical adversarial action against a named defendant. It is a non-contentious judicial matter—a determination request (Civil Procedure Code Art. 382 et seq.).

Which court is competent and has jurisdiction?

The competent court is the Peace Civil Court. Jurisdiction generally lies with the court of the deceased’s last place of residence (TCC Art. 589; CPC Art. 11).

Is there a specific limitation period to file?

There is no special peremptory period or statute of limitations prescribed solely for this action. In practice, filing after the estate opens and before partition—when protection is needed—is particularly important.

What should be prepared before filing?

Preparing the following documents often facilitates the process:

  • death certificate;
  • population registry extract;
  • certificate of heirship (if any);
  • known immovable property information;
  • information on bank and vehicle records.

How does the proceeding progress?

Typical stages include:

  1. Application to the Peace Civil Court.
  2. The court requests records from public authorities.
  3. Title, bank and vehicle records are reviewed.
  4. Expert examination is conducted if needed.
  5. Estate components are identified.
  6. The estate schedule is prepared and the court decides.

The court may also order sealing or other protective measures where appropriate (TCC Art. 589).

How long does it take to conclude?

Depending on the size of the estate, cases often conclude within roughly 3 to 12 months in practice. Delays may be due to large estates, cross-border inquiries, or waiting for bank records.
