What Is an Estate Determination Case? How Is It Opened?

Home Legal Areas Inheritance Law Estate Determination Case

In this article, we aim to provide core and clear information about the estate determination cases that come to the agenda in cases where the assets left after the deceased's passing are not fully known, there is hesitation among the heirs regarding the assets included in the estate, or the scope of the assets needs to be clarified before the inheritance sharing. This case is one of the legal possibilities for the protection of the estate within the scope of Article 589 of the Turkish Civil Code. In this context, most frequently asked questions in practice are addressed, such as what an estate determination case is, in which situations it can be filed, by whom it can be filed, which court is in charge, and how the litigation process progresses.

What is an estate determination case?

An estate determination case is a determination request carried out for the purpose of determining and protecting the assets and debts left after the deceased's passing. With this case, real estates, bank accounts, vehicle records, receivables, debts, and other asset elements are investigated and recorded by the court. This matter is based on the regulation regarding the fact that the peace judge can take the necessary measures for the protection of the estate within the scope of Turkish Civil Code Art. 589.

In which situations is an estate determination case filed?

An estate determination case is filed specifically in the following situations:

  • If the deceased's assets are not fully known
  • If there is a dispute among the heirs
  • If there is a suspicion of asset smuggling
  • If the estate assets need to be protected
  • If the assets are desired to be clarified before the sharing is made

This case is in the nature of a protection and determination measure.

Who can file an estate determination case?

This case can generally be filed by:

  • Legal heirs
  • Appointed heirs
  • Legatees
  • Estate creditors

In addition, the peace judge can also act ex officio (automatically) if deemed necessary.

Against whom is an estate determination case filed?

An estate determination case is not a classical adversarial case filed against a defendant. This case is a determination request in the nature of non-contentious jurisdiction (Code of Civil Procedure Art. 382 et seq.).

What is the competent and authorized court in an estate determination case?

The competent court is the Peace Civil Court. The authorized court is the court of the deceased's last place of residence (TCC Art. 589, CCP Art. 11).

Is there a time limit for filing an estate determination case?

There is no specific peremptory period or statute of limitations determined in the law for the estate determination case. However, in practice, it is important to file it after the opening of the inheritance, when the need for the protection of the estate arises, and before the sharing is made.

What should be done before filing an estate determination case?

Preparing the following documents before the case is filed generally facilitates the process:

  • Death certificate
  • Population registry extract
  • Certificate of inheritance (if any)
  • Known real estate information
  • Information regarding bank and vehicle records

How does the estate determination litigation process progress?

The estate determination case generally consists of the following stages:

  1. An application is made to the Peace Civil Court.
  2. The court requests records from official institutions.
  3. Title deed, bank, and vehicle records are investigated.
  4. An expert examination is carried out if necessary.
  5. Asset elements regarding the estate are determined.
  6. An estate book is created and a decision is made.

If the court deems it necessary, it can also take additional measures regarding the sealing or protection of the estate (TCC Art. 589).

How long does it take for the estate determination case to conclude?

Although it varies according to the scope of the estate, it is generally concluded between 3 months and 12 months in practice.

The prolongation of the period is mostly due to reasons such as the assets being high, the search abroad being carried out, and the waiting for the bank records.
